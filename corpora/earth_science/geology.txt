Plate tectonics describes the movement of Earth lithospheric plates on the asthenosphere.
The Earth crust is divided into about 15 major tectonic plates that move a few centimeters per year.
Divergent boundaries occur where plates move apart and new crust forms from rising magma.
Convergent boundaries occur where plates collide causing subduction mountain building or volcanic arcs.
Transform boundaries occur where plates slide horizontally past each other causing earthquakes.
The mid-Atlantic ridge is a divergent boundary where the Atlantic Ocean floor is spreading apart.
The Ring of Fire encircles the Pacific Ocean and contains 75 percent of the world active volcanoes.
Earthquakes occur when stress along fault lines is released as seismic waves.
The Richter scale and moment magnitude scale measure earthquake energy release.
Seismic waves include primary P waves secondary S waves and surface waves.
P waves are compressional longitudinal waves that travel through solids and liquids.
S waves are shear transverse waves that cannot travel through liquids.
The Mohorovicic discontinuity marks the boundary between the crust and upper mantle.
Earth interior consists of the crust mantle outer core of liquid iron and inner core of solid iron.
Convection currents in the mantle drive the movement of tectonic plates.
Minerals are naturally occurring inorganic solids with a definite chemical composition and crystal structure.
Igneous rocks form from the cooling and solidification of magma or lava.
Sedimentary rocks form from the accumulation and cementation of sediments.
Metamorphic rocks form when existing rocks are transformed by heat pressure or chemical processes.
The rock cycle describes the continuous transformation between igneous sedimentary and metamorphic rocks.
Fossils are preserved remains or traces of organisms from past geological ages.
Radioactive dating uses the decay of isotopes like carbon-14 and uranium-238 to determine rock ages.
The geologic time scale divides Earth 4.6 billion year history into eons eras periods and epochs.
Pangaea was a supercontinent that began breaking apart approximately 200 million years ago.
Weathering is the breakdown of rocks at Earth surface by physical chemical and biological processes.
Erosion transports weathered material by water wind ice and gravity.
Soil formation depends on parent material climate organisms topography and time.
Volcanoes erupt when magma from the mantle reaches the surface through vents in the crust.
Hotspots are volcanic regions not at plate boundaries caused by mantle plumes like Hawaii.
The Himalaya mountains formed from the collision of the Indian and Eurasian tectonic plates.
