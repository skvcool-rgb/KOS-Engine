Cosmology is the study of the origin structure evolution and ultimate fate of the universe.
The Big Bang theory states the universe began as an extremely hot dense state approximately 13.8 billion years ago.
The cosmic microwave background radiation is the thermal afterglow of the Big Bang detected at 2.7 Kelvin.
The cosmic microwave background was first detected by Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson in 1965.
The universe is expanding and the rate of expansion is described by the Hubble constant.
Edwin Hubble discovered in 1929 that galaxies are moving away from us with velocities proportional to their distances.
Dark matter does not emit or absorb light but exerts gravitational effects and comprises approximately 27 percent of the universe.
Dark energy is a mysterious force driving the accelerating expansion of the universe comprising approximately 68 percent.
Ordinary matter makes up only about 5 percent of the total energy content of the universe.
Galaxy rotation curves provide evidence for dark matter as outer stars orbit faster than expected from visible mass alone.
Gravitational lensing of background galaxies by foreground clusters provides further evidence for dark matter.
The observable universe has a diameter of approximately 93 billion light-years.
There are approximately two trillion galaxies in the observable universe.
The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy approximately 100000 light-years in diameter containing 100 to 400 billion stars.
The Andromeda Galaxy is the nearest large galaxy to the Milky Way at approximately 2.5 million light-years away.
Galaxy clusters are the largest gravitationally bound structures in the universe.
Cosmic inflation is a rapid exponential expansion of the universe that occurred within the first fraction of a second after the Big Bang.
Inflation explains the uniformity and flatness of the observable universe.
Nucleosynthesis in the first few minutes after the Big Bang produced hydrogen helium and trace lithium.
Stars form from the gravitational collapse of dense regions within molecular clouds of gas and dust.
The Hertzsprung-Russell diagram classifies stars by luminosity and temperature.
Main-sequence stars fuse hydrogen into helium and spend most of their lives in this phase.
Red giant stars have exhausted their core hydrogen and expanded to many times their original size.
White dwarfs are the remnant cores of low-mass stars with roughly the mass of the Sun compressed to Earth size.
Neutron stars are extremely dense remnants of massive star supernovae with diameters of about 20 kilometers.
A supernova is the explosive death of a massive star that briefly outshines an entire galaxy.
Type Ia supernovae are used as standard candles to measure cosmic distances and led to the discovery of dark energy.
Black holes form when massive stars collapse and their gravity prevents even light from escaping.
Supermassive black holes with millions to billions of solar masses reside at the centers of most galaxies.
The James Webb Space Telescope launched in 2021 observes the universe in infrared to study the earliest galaxies.
