Biochemistry studies the chemical processes and substances within living organisms.
Proteins are polymers of amino acids linked by peptide bonds.
There are twenty standard amino acids used by all living organisms to build proteins.
The primary structure of a protein is its linear sequence of amino acids.
The secondary structure includes alpha helices and beta sheets stabilized by hydrogen bonds.
The tertiary structure is the three-dimensional folding of a protein determined by side chain interactions.
The quaternary structure describes the arrangement of multiple protein subunits.
Enzymes are biological catalysts that accelerate chemical reactions by lowering activation energy.
The active site of an enzyme binds the substrate with high specificity.
Michaelis-Menten kinetics describes enzyme reaction rates as a function of substrate concentration.
Allosteric regulation modifies enzyme activity by binding molecules at sites other than the active site.
Carbohydrates are sugars and starches that serve as energy sources and structural components.
Glucose is a six-carbon monosaccharide that is the primary energy source for cells.
Glycolysis breaks down glucose into two molecules of pyruvate producing ATP and NADH.
The citric acid cycle oxidizes acetyl-CoA to produce CO2 NADH FADH2 and GTP.
The electron transport chain uses NADH and FADH2 to generate a proton gradient that drives ATP synthesis.
Oxidative phosphorylation produces approximately 30 to 32 ATP molecules per glucose molecule.
ATP adenosine triphosphate is the primary energy currency of all living cells.
Lipids are hydrophobic molecules including fats oils and phospholipids.
Phospholipids form bilayers that are the structural basis of all cell membranes.
Fatty acids can be saturated with no double bonds or unsaturated with one or more double bonds.
Cholesterol is a lipid that maintains membrane fluidity and is a precursor to steroid hormones.
Nucleotides are the building blocks of DNA and RNA consisting of a base sugar and phosphate group.
The four DNA bases are adenine thymine guanine and cytosine.
The four RNA bases are adenine uracil guanine and cytosine with uracil replacing thymine.
DNA double helix is held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs A-T and G-C.
Coenzymes are organic molecules that assist enzymes including NAD FAD and coenzyme A.
Vitamins are essential organic compounds that often function as coenzymes or their precursors.
Metabolism encompasses all chemical reactions in an organism including catabolism and anabolism.
Signal transduction pathways relay chemical signals from the cell surface to intracellular targets.
