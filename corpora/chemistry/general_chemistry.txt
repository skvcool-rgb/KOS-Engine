An atom consists of a nucleus containing protons and neutrons surrounded by electrons in orbitals.
The atomic number defines an element and equals the number of protons in its nucleus.
Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.
The periodic table organizes elements by increasing atomic number and groups them by chemical properties.
Electronegativity measures the tendency of an atom to attract shared electrons in a chemical bond.
Fluorine is the most electronegative element on the periodic table.
Ionic bonds form when electrons are transferred from a metal to a nonmetal.
Covalent bonds form when atoms share electron pairs.
Metallic bonds involve a sea of delocalized electrons shared among metal atoms in a lattice.
Hydrogen bonds are intermolecular forces between a hydrogen atom bonded to nitrogen oxygen or fluorine and a nearby electronegative atom.
Van der Waals forces are weak intermolecular attractions that arise from temporary dipoles.
The octet rule states that atoms tend to form bonds to achieve eight electrons in their valence shell.
Oxidation is the loss of electrons and reduction is the gain of electrons.
An oxidizing agent gains electrons and is reduced during a chemical reaction.
A reducing agent loses electrons and is oxidized during a chemical reaction.
The mole is defined as exactly 6.02214076 times 10 to the 23rd particles.
Avogadro number equals 6.022 times 10 to the 23rd and represents the number of atoms in one mole of a substance.
Molar mass is the mass of one mole of a substance measured in grams per mole.
Chemical equilibrium occurs when the rates of forward and reverse reactions are equal.
Le Chatelier principle states that a system at equilibrium shifts to counteract any imposed change.
The pH scale measures the hydrogen ion concentration with pH 7 being neutral below 7 acidic and above 7 basic.
Acids donate protons according to the Bronsted-Lowry definition.
Bases accept protons according to the Bronsted-Lowry definition.
Buffer solutions resist changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added.
Catalysts increase reaction rates by lowering the activation energy without being consumed.
Activation energy is the minimum energy required for a chemical reaction to proceed.
The Arrhenius equation relates reaction rate to temperature and activation energy.
Exothermic reactions release heat to the surroundings and have a negative enthalpy change.
Endothermic reactions absorb heat from the surroundings and have a positive enthalpy change.
Hess law states that the total enthalpy change of a reaction is independent of the pathway taken.
