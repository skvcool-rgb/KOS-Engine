Linear algebra studies vector spaces and linear transformations between them.
A vector is a mathematical object with magnitude and direction in a vector space.
A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers that represents a linear transformation.
Matrix multiplication is not commutative meaning AB generally does not equal BA.
The determinant of a matrix is a scalar that indicates whether the matrix is invertible.
A matrix is invertible if and only if its determinant is nonzero.
The identity matrix is a square matrix with ones on the diagonal and zeros elsewhere.
Eigenvalues and eigenvectors describe how a linear transformation scales vectors along specific directions.
The characteristic equation det(A minus lambda I) equals zero yields the eigenvalues of matrix A.
The rank of a matrix equals the number of linearly independent rows or columns.
A system of linear equations can be solved using Gaussian elimination row reduction.
The null space of a matrix is the set of all vectors that map to the zero vector.
The column space of a matrix is the set of all possible outputs of the linear transformation.
Orthogonal vectors are perpendicular and their dot product equals zero.
The dot product of two vectors equals the product of their magnitudes times the cosine of the angle between them.
The cross product of two vectors produces a vector perpendicular to both with magnitude equal to the parallelogram area.
Singular value decomposition factors any matrix into three matrices U Sigma and V transpose.
Principal component analysis uses eigenvectors to identify directions of maximum variance in data.
The spectral theorem states that symmetric matrices have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors.
A positive definite matrix has all positive eigenvalues and defines an ellipsoidal quadratic form.
The trace of a matrix equals the sum of its diagonal elements and equals the sum of its eigenvalues.
The Gram-Schmidt process converts a set of linearly independent vectors into an orthonormal basis.
A linear transformation preserves vector addition and scalar multiplication.
The dimension of a vector space equals the number of vectors in any basis.
The rank-nullity theorem states that rank plus nullity equals the number of columns of a matrix.
Tensor products extend linear algebra to multilinear maps and higher-dimensional arrays.
The Cayley-Hamilton theorem states that every square matrix satisfies its own characteristic equation.
Diagonalization expresses a matrix as a product of its eigenvector matrix diagonal eigenvalue matrix and inverse eigenvector matrix.
Hermitian matrices are the complex analog of symmetric matrices with real eigenvalues.
The condition number of a matrix measures its sensitivity to numerical perturbations.
