Special relativity is based on two postulates that the laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames and the speed of light is constant.
Time dilation causes moving clocks to tick slower relative to a stationary observer.
Length contraction causes objects moving at high speed to appear shorter along the direction of motion.
The Lorentz transformation relates space and time coordinates between two inertial reference frames.
Mass-energy equivalence is expressed by Einstein famous equation E equals mc squared.
Nothing with mass can travel at or faster than the speed of light in vacuum.
The speed of light in vacuum is exactly 299792458 meters per second.
Relativistic momentum increases without bound as an object approaches the speed of light.
The twin paradox illustrates that a traveling twin ages less than a stationary twin due to time dilation.
General relativity describes gravity as the curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy.
The Einstein field equations relate the geometry of spacetime to the distribution of matter and energy.
Massive objects like stars and planets curve spacetime around them causing other objects to follow curved paths.
Gravitational time dilation causes clocks in stronger gravitational fields to tick slower.
Gravitational lensing bends light from distant objects as it passes through curved spacetime near massive objects.
Black holes are regions where spacetime curvature becomes so extreme that nothing can escape including light.
The Schwarzschild radius defines the event horizon of a non-rotating black hole.
Hawking radiation is thermal radiation predicted to be emitted by black holes due to quantum effects near the event horizon.
Gravitational waves are ripples in spacetime produced by accelerating massive objects.
LIGO detected gravitational waves for the first time in 2015 from merging black holes confirming Einstein prediction.
The equivalence principle states that gravitational and inertial mass are identical.
Frame dragging causes rotating massive objects to drag spacetime around them.
The cosmological constant represents dark energy that drives the accelerating expansion of the universe.
GPS satellites must account for both special and general relativistic time dilation to maintain accuracy.
Geodesics are the straightest possible paths through curved spacetime.
The precession of Mercury orbit was one of the first confirmations of general relativity.
Neutron stars are extremely dense remnants of stellar collapse with intense gravitational fields.
Pulsars are rapidly rotating neutron stars that emit beams of electromagnetic radiation.
Time travel to the future is physically possible through time dilation at high velocities or strong gravitational fields.
The Friedmann equations describe the expansion of the universe within general relativity.
The Big Bang theory states that the universe expanded from an extremely hot and dense initial state approximately 13.8 billion years ago.
