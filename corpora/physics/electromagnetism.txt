Electric charge comes in two types positive and negative and like charges repel while opposite charges attract.
Coulomb law states that the electrostatic force between two charges is proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance.
The electric field at a point is the force per unit positive charge placed at that point.
Gauss law states that the total electric flux through a closed surface equals the enclosed charge divided by the permittivity of free space.
Electric potential is the work done per unit charge to move a charge from infinity to a point in the field.
Capacitance is the ability of a system to store electric charge per unit voltage.
A parallel plate capacitor has capacitance proportional to the plate area and inversely proportional to the separation distance.
Dielectrics increase capacitance by reducing the electric field between the plates.
Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge measured in amperes.
Ohm law states that voltage equals current times resistance.
Resistance in a wire is proportional to its length and inversely proportional to its cross-sectional area.
Resistivity depends on the material and temperature of the conductor.
Kirchhoff current law states that the total current entering a junction equals the total current leaving.
Kirchhoff voltage law states that the sum of voltage drops around any closed loop equals zero.
A magnetic field is produced by moving electric charges and exerts forces on other moving charges.
The Biot-Savart law calculates the magnetic field produced by a current-carrying wire.
Ampere law relates the magnetic field around a closed loop to the current passing through the loop.
Faraday law of induction states that a changing magnetic flux through a loop induces an electromotive force.
Lenz law states that an induced current flows in a direction to oppose the change in flux that produced it.
Maxwell equations unify electricity and magnetism into a single framework of four equations.
Electromagnetic waves travel at the speed of light and consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other.
The electromagnetic spectrum ranges from radio waves through microwaves infrared visible light ultraviolet X-rays to gamma rays.
The Lorentz force describes the force on a charged particle moving through electric and magnetic fields.
Inductance measures the tendency of a conductor to oppose changes in current flowing through it.
An LC circuit oscillates at a resonant frequency determined by the inductance and capacitance.
Electromagnetic radiation carries energy and momentum and exerts radiation pressure on surfaces.
Superconductors have zero electrical resistance below a critical temperature.
The Meissner effect causes superconductors to expel magnetic fields from their interior.
The Hall effect produces a voltage across a conductor when a magnetic field is applied perpendicular to the current flow.
Eddy currents are loops of electric current induced within conductors by changing magnetic fields.
