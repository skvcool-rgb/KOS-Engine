Newton first law states that an object at rest stays at rest and an object in motion stays in motion unless acted upon by an external force.
Newton second law states that force equals mass multiplied by acceleration, expressed as F equals ma.
Newton third law states that for every action there is an equal and opposite reaction.
Momentum is the product of an object mass and its velocity and is conserved in closed systems.
Kinetic energy equals one half times mass times velocity squared.
Potential energy due to gravity equals mass times gravitational acceleration times height.
The work-energy theorem states that net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy.
Angular momentum is conserved in the absence of external torques.
Torque is the rotational equivalent of force and equals force times the lever arm distance.
The moment of inertia determines how much torque is needed to achieve angular acceleration.
Friction is a contact force that opposes relative motion between two surfaces.
Static friction is generally stronger than kinetic friction for the same pair of surfaces.
The coefficient of friction depends on the materials of both surfaces in contact.
Hooke law states that the force exerted by a spring is proportional to its displacement from equilibrium.
Simple harmonic motion occurs when the restoring force is proportional to displacement.
The period of a simple pendulum depends on its length and gravitational acceleration but not its mass.
Centripetal acceleration keeps an object moving in a circular path and is directed toward the center.
The gravitational force between two masses is proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
Escape velocity from Earth surface is approximately 11.2 kilometers per second.
Kepler first law states that planets orbit the Sun in ellipses with the Sun at one focus.
Kepler second law states that a line from a planet to the Sun sweeps equal areas in equal times.
Kepler third law relates the square of the orbital period to the cube of the semi-major axis.
The conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed only transformed between forms.
Elastic collisions conserve both momentum and kinetic energy.
Inelastic collisions conserve momentum but not kinetic energy.
Terminal velocity occurs when the drag force on a falling object equals the gravitational force.
Projectile motion can be decomposed into independent horizontal and vertical components.
The Coriolis effect deflects moving objects on rotating reference frames like Earth.
Lagrangian mechanics reformulates classical mechanics using the difference of kinetic and potential energy.
Hamiltonian mechanics describes a system using generalized coordinates and momenta.
