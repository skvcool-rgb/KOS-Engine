An ecosystem consists of all living organisms and their physical environment interacting as a system.
Producers are organisms like plants and algae that convert sunlight into chemical energy through photosynthesis.
Consumers are organisms that obtain energy by eating other organisms.
Decomposers break down dead organic matter and recycle nutrients back into the ecosystem.
A food chain traces the flow of energy from producers through consumers to decomposers.
Food webs are complex networks of interconnected food chains in an ecosystem.
Trophic levels represent positions in a food chain with producers at the first level.
The ten percent rule states that approximately 10 percent of energy transfers between trophic levels.
Keystone species have a disproportionately large effect on their ecosystem relative to their abundance.
Apex predators regulate prey populations and maintain ecosystem balance through top-down control.
Biodiversity is the variety of life at genetic species and ecosystem levels.
Tropical rainforests contain the highest biodiversity of any terrestrial biome.
Coral reefs support approximately 25 percent of all marine species despite covering less than 1 percent of the ocean floor.
The nitrogen cycle converts atmospheric nitrogen into forms usable by organisms through fixation nitrification and denitrification.
The carbon cycle moves carbon between the atmosphere oceans land organisms and geological formations.
The phosphorus cycle moves phosphorus from rocks through soil water and organisms.
The water cycle includes evaporation transpiration condensation precipitation and runoff.
Primary succession occurs on bare surfaces with no existing soil like new volcanic rock.
Secondary succession occurs in areas where a disturbance has removed existing vegetation but soil remains.
Ecological niches define the role and position of a species in its environment including resources and interactions.
Competitive exclusion principle states that two species competing for the same resources cannot coexist indefinitely.
Mutualism is an interaction where both species benefit such as pollinators and flowering plants.
Parasitism is an interaction where one organism benefits at the expense of the host.
Commensalism is an interaction where one species benefits while the other is unaffected.
Invasive species are non-native organisms that cause ecological or economic harm in their introduced range.
Habitat fragmentation divides continuous habitats into smaller isolated patches reducing biodiversity.
The sixth mass extinction is the ongoing rapid loss of species primarily caused by human activities.
Conservation biology applies scientific principles to protect and restore biodiversity.
Protected areas like national parks and marine reserves safeguard critical habitats and species.
Ecosystem services are the benefits humans derive from ecosystems including clean water pollination and climate regulation.
