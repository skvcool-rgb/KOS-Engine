Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole including national output employment and price levels.
Gross domestic product GDP measures the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country.
GDP can be calculated by the expenditure approach as consumption plus investment plus government spending plus net exports.
Real GDP adjusts for inflation and measures output in constant prices to track actual economic growth.
The GDP growth rate indicates the pace of economic expansion or contraction.
Unemployment rate is the percentage of the labor force that is actively seeking but unable to find work.
Frictional unemployment occurs when workers are between jobs and searching for new employment.
Structural unemployment results from a mismatch between worker skills and job requirements.
Cyclical unemployment rises during economic recessions and falls during expansions.
Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level measured by indices like the Consumer Price Index.
Hyperinflation is extremely rapid inflation that destroys a currency purchasing power.
Deflation is a sustained decrease in the general price level that can discourage spending and investment.
The Phillips curve suggests an inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment in the short run.
Fiscal policy uses government spending and taxation to influence the economy.
Expansionary fiscal policy increases government spending or cuts taxes to stimulate economic growth.
Contractionary fiscal policy decreases spending or raises taxes to reduce inflation.
Monetary policy is conducted by central banks to manage interest rates and money supply.
The Federal Reserve is the central bank of the United States and sets the federal funds rate.
Lowering interest rates encourages borrowing and spending stimulating economic activity.
Raising interest rates discourages borrowing and spending reducing inflationary pressure.
Quantitative easing is when central banks purchase financial assets to increase money supply and lower interest rates.
The money multiplier describes how an initial deposit creates a larger increase in the total money supply through lending.
Exchange rates determine the value of one currency relative to another and affect international trade.
A trade deficit occurs when a country imports exceed its exports.
Comparative advantage states that countries benefit from specializing in goods they produce at the lowest opportunity cost.
The business cycle consists of expansion peak contraction and trough phases.
A recession is commonly defined as two consecutive quarters of declining real GDP.
The national debt is the total amount of money a government owes to creditors.
Supply-side economics argues that lower taxes and reduced regulation stimulate economic growth.
Keynesian economics advocates government intervention through fiscal policy to manage aggregate demand.
